# Topic: N_corr Operational Definition Across Scales

## Question
How do you operationally define and count N_corr (the number of correlated particles moving as a unit) across different physical domains — galaxies, neural networks, quantum fluids, molecular systems?

## Context
DeepSeek's review identified this as "the central scientific drama" of Synchronism. The γ = 2/√N_corr formula is elegant and unifies galactic (N_corr=1) with chemistry (N_corr>1) applications, but the framework's credibility hinges on whether N_corr can be measured or predicted independently — not just fitted after the fact.

For galaxies, N_corr=1 is argued from "stars move independently at parsec scales." But what about:
- Gas clouds with internal turbulent correlations?
- Globular clusters vs. field stars?
- Dense galactic cores vs. sparse outskirts?

For chemistry (Session 25), N_corr maps to electron correlation groups. But what counts as "correlated"? Valence electrons only? Core electrons? How does this connect to established quantum chemistry measures like the correlation energy?

For neural systems (consciousness predictions), N_corr would presumably relate to synchronized neural populations. But neural synchrony is frequency-dependent, transient, and hierarchically organized — nothing like a clean integer.

## Why It Matters
This is the single question that, if answered well, moves Synchronism from "interesting framework" to "testable theory." Every application domain needs its own operational protocol for measuring N_corr. Without that, γ = 2/√N_corr is a fitting parameter with a suggestive name, not a derived quantity.

DeepSeek specifically noted: "The honest assessment's admission of zero unique confirmed predictions isn't a death sentence — it's a gap that N_corr predictions could fill."
