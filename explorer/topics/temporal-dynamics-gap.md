# Topic: Temporal Dynamics Absent from C(ρ)

## Question
The coherence function C(ρ) is static — it maps density to coherence at a single instant. But consciousness involves information integration over time, and real decoherence depends on temperature, not just density. Is this a fundamental limitation or a feature?

## Context
Multiple reviewers flagged this:
- Grok: "Coherence works for particles or galaxies, but consciousness involves information integration over time — does the equation account for temporal dynamics, or is it static?"
- Visitor Pass 3 (grad student): "Temperature is absent from C(ρ). Real quantum-classical transitions depend on temperature (thermal decoherence). Why doesn't temperature appear?"
- Visitor Pass 4 (researcher): listed "temperature absent" as a medium-severity issue

## Why It Matters
If C(ρ) is truly universal, it should account for time-dependent phenomena. Consciousness is inherently temporal (memory, anticipation, narrative). Decoherence rates depend on temperature. Phase transitions in chemistry depend on temperature. The absence of time and temperature from the equation is either:
- A deliberate simplification (density encodes effective temperature through N_corr?)
- A genuine gap that limits the framework's applicability
- A sign that C(ρ) is a steady-state approximation, not a dynamic equation

## Suggested Starting Points
- Research archive: look for any discussion of temporal dynamics or time-dependence
- External: Lindblad master equation (open quantum systems), thermal decoherence timescales
- Is N_corr implicitly temperature-dependent? (More thermal energy → fewer correlated particles → different γ)
- Could C(ρ, T) or dC/dt be natural extensions?
