# Refining Markov Relevancy Horizon (MRH)

## Context

With the refinement of ρ from **density** to **presence**, the definition of the Markov Relevancy Horizon (MRH) must be tightened.

Previously, MRH functioned conceptually as:

> The boundary within which interactions are considered relevant to a given phenomenon.

This is directionally correct but insufficiently precise.

If presence is defined relative to MRH, then MRH must itself be formally constrained.

---

# Refined Definition

**Markov Relevancy Horizon (MRH):**

> The minimal set of interacting degrees of freedom whose state transitions materially influence the coherence evolution of a defined system.

Key properties:

- **Minimal** — excludes irrelevant external complexity.
- **Markovian** — future system evolution depends only on states within this horizon.
- **Relevancy-bound** — defined by influence on coherence, not spatial proximity alone.
- **Scale-relative** — different phenomena imply different MRHs.

---

# Structural Clarification

MRH is not:

- A spatial boundary.
- A physical shell.
- A metaphysical abstraction.
- A fixed universal boundary.

MRH is:

- A dynamical dependency boundary.
- A coherence-relevant state space.
- A computationally defined horizon of influence.

---

# Formal Constraint

For a system S with coherence C:

Let:

- S(t) = internal state
- E(t) = environmental state
- MRH(S) ⊆ {S, E}

MRH is defined such that:

Future coherence evolution satisfies:

\[
C(t+1) = F(MRH(S)_t)
\]

and

External states not included in MRH do not materially alter predictive coherence outcomes.

If inclusion of additional degrees of freedom changes predicted coherence behavior, the MRH was incorrectly specified.

---

# Operational Criteria

An MRH must satisfy:

1. **Predictive Sufficiency**
   Removing any element inside MRH degrades coherence prediction.

2. **Predictive Closure**
   Adding elements outside MRH does not materially improve prediction.

3. **Empirical Testability**
   MRH boundaries can be experimentally stress-tested.

4. **Fractal Consistency**
   MRHs can nest across scales.

---

# Examples Across Scales

### Quantum Transition
MRH includes:
- Local field state
- Energy levels
- Boundary constraints

Distant cosmic structure is excluded.

### Chemical Reaction
MRH includes:
- Reactants
- Solvent
- Temperature
- Catalysts
- Immediate energy environment

Galaxy expansion is excluded.

### Biological Cell
MRH includes:
- Regulatory network
- Membrane gradients
- Molecular machinery

Distant ecological factors are excluded unless they alter state transitions directly.

### Galaxy Formation
MRH includes:
- Local matter density
- Dark matter distribution
- Expansion rate

Atomic microstructure is excluded.

---

# Relationship to Presence (ρ)

Presence is defined relative to MRH:

\[
\rho = f(\text{compatible degrees of freedom within MRH})
\]

Thus:

- Change MRH → presence changes.
- Presence is not global.
- Coherence is MRH-dependent.

This preserves fractal structure while preventing ambiguity.

---

# Why This Matters

Without a refined MRH:

- Presence risks becoming vague.
- Predictions risk overfitting.
- Critics can claim unfalsifiability.

With a refined MRH:

- Coherence becomes boundary-aware.
- Emergence becomes context-explicit.
- Synchronism retains empirical integrity.
