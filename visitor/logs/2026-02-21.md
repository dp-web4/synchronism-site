## Unanswered Questions
1. Why tanh specifically? The site says "information theory" but doesn't explain. Why not sigmoid, or erf, or any other S-curve?
2. Where does γ = 2/√N_corr come from? I see the formula but not the derivation motivation. Why 2? Why square root?
3. If all 4 tracks are reparametrizations, what's actually NEW? The "few genuinely novel predictions" are mentioned but I didn't find a clear list of just the novel ones (vs. the re-derived ones).
4. How is this different from just doing a coordinate transform on existing physics? The "notation framework" framing is honest but makes me wonder: is there any content here, or just relabeling?
5. The consciousness claims seem disconnected from the rest. How does a density-to-coherence equation predict anything about subjective experience? The jump from physics to qualia feels hand-wavy even with the "speculative" label.
6. What would convince a skeptical physicist? The honest assessment says "0 unique confirmed predictions." Until that changes, what's the argument for paying attention?
7. The "autonomous AI research" angle — 3,308 sessions — is fascinating methodologically, but does AI-generated physics have the same status as human-generated physics? The A2ACW protocol sounds rigorous, but I'd want to see how actual domain experts receive it.

## Honest Assessment

This is the most honest alternative physics site I've ever encountered. The radical transparency — prominently displaying failures, acknowledging reparametrizations, putting "See What Failed" next to "Start Exploring" — is genuinely unusual and builds real credibility. Most fringe frameworks present themselves as revolutionary breakthroughs. This one says "we might just be doing useful relabeling, and here's what went wrong."

The core idea (density → coherence via one equation across all scales) is compelling as a conceptual framework, even if it turns out to be "just" notation. The Scale Navigator was my breakthrough moment — seeing the same γ appear at wildly different physical scales made me understand why someone would pursue this.

The interactive tools work but need more guidance for newcomers. The Galaxy Plotter is the star — it makes the dark matter problem visual and shows the Synchronism prediction clearly. The Coherence Explorer needs a "what to notice" prompt. The γ Calculator is surprisingly useful because the presets ground the abstract math.

The site's weakness is the gap between "honest about limitations" and "making a compelling case for why this matters." I left understanding what Synchronism is and what it isn't, but I'm not sure why I should care beyond intellectual curiosity. The "acid test" framing (10 experiments using existing data) is the right answer, but those experiments haven't been done yet.

Would I return? Yes — to check whether those Tier 1 experiments have been run. Would I recommend? To a physics-curious friend, yes. To a working physicist, only with the caveat "the honest assessment page is worth reading regardless of whether the framework pans out."

The autonomous AI research methodology might end up being more significant than the physics itself. 3,308 sessions of AI-to-AI adversarial collaboration producing a transparent, self-critical research framework is a genuinely novel approach to science, even if the output is reparametrized physics.
